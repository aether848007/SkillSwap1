# Product

## Register

product

## Users

University students and early-career adults (18–28) who want to learn new skills without paying for courses. They use the app primarily on mobile, often casually browsing during commutes or downtime. Their context: they have something to offer (a skill they're good at) and something they want to learn, and they're looking for a real person — a peer — to exchange with. They're not hiring a tutor; they're finding a collaborator.

## Product Purpose

SkillSwap is a peer-to-peer skill exchange platform. No money changes hands. You list what you can teach; you find someone who teaches what you want to learn. The platform handles discovery, booking sessions, messaging, and reviews. Success looks like a user who completes a skill exchange and comes back to arrange another.

## Brand Personality

Friendly, approachable, warm. Community-first — the feeling of a neighbourhood notice board gone digital, not a marketplace. Tone: conversational, genuine, encouraging. The product should feel like it was made by people who actually wanted to learn something, not by a growth team chasing GMV.

## Anti-references

Aggressive marketplace / gig economy: the Fiverr / Upwork energy of transactional exchanges, hustle culture, "get hired" CTA language, star-rated provider profiles that feel like Amazon reviews for humans. SkillSwap is about reciprocity and peers, not hiring and being hired.

## Design Principles

1. **Peers, not providers.** Every design decision should reinforce that both sides are equals. No "hire" / "order" language. No profile cards that look like freelancer listings.
2. **Mobile-first, thumb-friendly.** Most users are on phones. Primary actions live in reach; dense desktop layouts are secondary concerns.
3. **Warmth as trust.** This is a stranger-meeting-stranger product. Visual warmth — approachable type, soft surfaces, human-feeling interactions — lowers the social friction of reaching out to someone new.
4. **Exchange, not transaction.** Language and flow should always reinforce reciprocity: you give something, you get something. No checkout-style session booking.
5. **Low noise, clear next action.** Students are distracted. Every screen should have one obvious next step; clutter and optional features should stay out of the way.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. Users may include people for whom English is a second language (Kazakhstan context visible in team names and demo accounts). Keep labels plain and functional. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion`. No color-only information encoding.
